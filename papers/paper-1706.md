### Abstract

In the era of rapid development of artificial intelligence and deep learning technologies， complex algorithms represented by large language models have led to an exponential growth in computing power demand. However， traditional electronic computing architectures are facing a dual dilemma： on one hand， semiconductor process nodes are approaching the physical limit，resulting in diminishing performance gains from transistor size reduction，while the cost and difficulty of each process upgrade continue to rise； on the other hand， the energy consumption problem of data centers has become increasingly acute， and the energy efficiency wall has emerged as a core obstacle hindering the continuous improvement of computing power. Against this backdrop， the paradigm of photonic computing， which uses light as the information carrier， has emerged. With its inherent capabilities of high-speed parallel processing，ultra-low energy consumption， and multi-dimensional information encoding， it offers a revolutionary solution to break through the computing power bottleneck. There exists an innate compatibility between optical computing and AI algorithms，rooted in the deep congruence of their computational paradigms. Core operations in neural networks， such as matrix multiplication and vector convolution， are essentially large-scale parallel processing tasks. The wave nature and spatial parallel propagation characteristics of photons provide an ideal physical implementation platform for these operations. Compared with the switching speed of electrons in semiconductor devices， optical signals can complete cross-dimensional information interaction within an extremely short time scale， and the energy consumption per bit of information processing is far lower than that of electronic devices. This technological advantage has reshaped the computing ecosystem in three key dimensions： in terms of computational efficiency， traditional computing processors based on the von Neumann architecture suffer from the inherent flaw of separating storage from computation， leading to a disproportionately high energy consumption for data transfer. In contrast， optical computing， through integrated photonic neural networks， can perform real-time matrix operations on optical signals on silicon photonics chips，effectively eliminating the data transfer bottleneck. Regarding energy efficiency ratio， photonic devices can process multiple optical signals in parallel on a single chip， achieving a significant improvement in energy efficiency ratio compared to GPUs in typical tasks like image recognition. This characteristic is of vital importance for energy-constrained scenarios such as edge computing and the Internet of Things. In terms of environmental adaptability，optical signal transmission is immune to electromagnetic interference， and its stability far xceeds that of electronic devices in extreme environments such as high temperatures and strong radiation，endowing it with irreplaceable advantages in demanding fields like aerospace and military communications. Currently， although traditional electronic computing still dominates the field of general-purpose computing power， optical computing， with its unique advantages， has become an indispensable part of heterogeneous computing architectures. The academic and industrial sectors are vigorously promoting the engineering implementation of photonic computing technology. From the continuous maturation of silicon photonics integration processes to the ongoing innovation of hybrid optoelectronic architectures， from the reakthroughs in the development of dedicated AI chips to the active exploration of photonic neuromorphic omputing， photonic computing is gradually transitioning from the theoretical verification stage to the critical period of commercial application. This comprehensive review traces the technological evolution of optical computing for neural network acceleration，systematically covering both linear and nonlinear computational frameworks. Within linear optical computing architectures， three core devices stand out with distinct advantages： Programmable Mach-Zehnder Interferometers enable large-scale reconfigurable matrix operations through precise phase modulation of optical signals，leveraging their inherent parallelism to adapt dynamically to varying neural network layer requirements. Micro-ring resonators， excel in multi-wavelength high-integration scenarios— their wavelength-division multiplexing capability allows simultaneous processing of multiple data streams on a single chip， achieving high-density parallelism with minimal crosstalk， a critical feature for compact photonic computing systems. Spatial diffractive optical elements harness wavefront manipulation to realize high spatial parallelism， executing ultra-large-scale vector convolutions essential for visual computing tasks without the constraints of waveguide losses，thus maximizing throughput in spatial information processing. In the realm of nonlinear optical computing， optical nonlinear activation functions emerge as the pivotal breakthrough for unlocking photonic neural network performance. Evolving from early hybrid optoelectronic implementations to cutting-edge all-optical solutions integrated on silicon photonics platforms， these functions now exhibit dynamic reconfigurability and ultra-low power consumption. This progression has overcome the fundamental limitation of linear optical systems in replicating the complex nonlinear behaviors of biological neural networks， significantly enhancing network expressivity and enabling more accurate emulation of brain-like computational paradigms. Nevertheless， the large-scale application of optical computing technology still faces numerous formidable challenges. At the level of optical devices， the current integration density and stability fail to meet the requirements of large-scale commercial applications， and issues such as coupling losses between devices，thermal stability， and long-term reliability urgently need to be addressed. During all-optical signal processing， noise control and precision optimization remain key technical difficulties restricting the performance improvement of optical computing. Additionally， the compatibility between optical computing systems and the existing electronic ecosystem cannot be overlooked. Achieving efficient data interaction and collaborative operation between optical and electronic computing is a crucial step in promoting the widespread application of optical computing technology. Looking ahead， with the continuous refinement of silicon photonics integration processes， the continuous innovation of hybrid optoelectronic architectures， and the in-depth advancement of interdisciplinary research， the deep integration and collaborative innovation of optical and electronic computing will surely become the key path to breaking through the computing power bottleneck and building green and efficient computing power infrastructure. Optical computing technology will comprehensively reshape the intelligent computing ecosystem， providing powerful computing support for the development of AI，playing a significant role in numerous fields such as autonomous driving，financial technology， and weather forecasting， and propelling human intelligent computing technology to a new stage of development. © 2025, Chinese Optical Society.
